Prologue

The “Second Nepal” fear and the demand for evidence

It started with a profound sense of disappointment and a very specific, looming dread.

In the digital halls of the r/Dominicanos Reddit community, a growing anxiety was taking shape: businesses were closing, fixed costs and debt felt mentally exhausting, and street-level consumption seemed noticeably weaker.

The overwhelming mood painted a picture of a suffocating reality: a fear that the Dominican Republic was drifting toward a “second Nepal,” understood here not as a prediction, but as an image of irreversible social and economic breakdown. Yet, at the exact same time, official macroeconomic data broadcasted a very different reality: stability, positive growth, rising formal employment, and record foreign direct investment.

This massive disconnect prompted a simple but strict challenge to the raw data: find the truth in the gap, and do not assume anything.

The rule of this investigation was to strip away both fatalism and political spin. Instead of beginning with a conclusion, we went to the numbers: official consumer price indices, food-basket pressure, formal job creation, tax collections, tourism receipts, credit, and signals of small-business stress.

The data did not deliver a neat, comfortable resolution, nor did it “prove everything.” Instead, it forced us into a highly specific, uncomfortable reality.

On one hand, the evidence did not support a broad macroeconomic freefall. The system is being strongly stabilized from above. Foreign air arrivals and estimated monetary inflows from tourism grew significantly, the formal employment channel remained supported, and formal workers showed signs of wage cushioning against inflation.

But on the other hand, the data also rejects a narrative of generalized comfort. The anxiety on the street does not appear to be simple hysteria: measurable pressure channels exist.

The food squeeze: while headline inflation was contained at 4.85%, food inflation ran much hotter at 7.28%, driven by culturally visible daily staples like garlic (+56.68%), green bananas (+32.99%), and fresh chicken (+13.66%).

Business stress: MIPYMES faced pressure from financing costs, signs of greater repayment stress, and foreign-exchange exposure. Ordinary commerce indicators also did not point to broad, uniform comfort.

Crucially, the investigation hit hard boundaries where the data simply could not prove that households are fully absorbing the pressure. We found no sufficient proof that informal workers or whole households are earning enough to offset the food squeeze. We also found no direct proof of how consumer behavior is adapting: whether households are buying less, substituting brands, or trading down in quality.

We went looking to test the fear of a total collapse. What we found was something else: an economy stabilized from above by strong external inflows, but squeezing domestic margins from below.

Not collapse. Not comfort. A selective squeeze.

Method note: this prologue explains the narrative origin of the investigation. The Reddit conversation is not treated as a representative survey. The article’s substantive claims rely on the sources, registries, and evidence boundaries linked throughout the publication.

Executive summary

Macroeconomic improvement does not settle the household question

The central reading is cautious: the Dominican Republic does not show, on this evidence, a generalized collapse. But the economy also cannot be described as comfortable for every household. The responsible signal is a selective squeeze, where some aggregate indicators improve while parts of basic consumption remain under pressure.

Tourism grows in both volume and monetary inflows. At the same time, food inflation runs above general inflation, and several essential products show increases far above the average. The key issue is transmission: more evidence is still needed to show how much of the stronger inflow becomes local household relief.

Evidence-protected thesis: this publication does not claim total crisis or broad comfort. It claims a selective squeeze, with positive tourism evidence, visible food-price pressure, and causal bridges that remain unproven.
