## The short version

The Dominican economy does not look like a collapse story.

But the evidence also does not justify a comfort story.

The strongest current reading is more specific and more uncomfortable:

**Not collapse. Not comfort. A selective squeeze.**

That means the economy can show resilience at the macro level while pressure still concentrates unevenly across households, informal workers, small businesses, and people exposed to food costs. It also means that some formal workers may have received enough wage cushioning to soften part of the shock, while many other groups remain harder to measure with the evidence currently available.

This article is not an argument that everything is falling apart. It is also not an argument that everything is fine. It is an evidence-bounded attempt to separate what can be shown from what is still missing.

## Why the usual debate is too simple

Public economic debates often collapse into two easy stories.

The first story says the economy is strong, so household pressure must be exaggerated.

The second story says people are struggling, so the economy must be collapsing.

The evidence reviewed in this project does not fit either extreme.

There are signs of macro resilience and formal labor cushioning. There are also signs of food pressure, tight household margins, uneven public cushioning, business-side stress, demand softness, and unresolved exposure among informal workers and households outside the formal wage channel.

The result is not a clean national comfort story. But it is not a collapse story either.

It is a selective squeeze.

## What the evidence supports

The strongest supported finding is that a formal-worker cushioning channel exists.

In simple terms, the wage-food offset evidence is strongest for workers captured in the formal TSS channel. That matters because formal payroll data can show whether some registered workers received wage gains large enough to offset certain food-pressure measures.

But this finding has a hard boundary: it applies to the formal channel only.

It cannot automatically describe informal workers. It cannot automatically describe all households. It cannot automatically describe poorer households, people outside payroll systems, or families whose income comes from unstable or mixed sources.

That distinction is not a technicality. It is the difference between a valid claim and an overclaim.

## What remains unresolved

The missing bridge is the household and informal-worker income bridge.

Current labor-market context exists, but labor context is not the same thing as current income or remuneration by household group. A job count, employment signal, or labor-market update can help describe the environment, but it does not automatically prove that households had enough income growth to offset price pressure.

The project also found useful near-current context from 2025 sources, including FMCG behavior context and official BCRD informal-income patterns. Those sources matter because they show the kind of behavior and income questions that should be asked.

But they cannot do the work of 2026 proof.

A 2025 behavior signal cannot prove current 2026 trade-down. A 2025 informal-income pattern cannot calculate a 2026 household offset. Those sources are context, not closure.

## The trade-down question is still open

One of the most important unresolved questions is whether households changed how they buy.

That means questions like:

- Are people buying smaller quantities?
- Are they switching to cheaper brands?
- Are they moving to private-label goods?
- Are they reducing categories?
- Are they buying less frequently?
- Are they changing basket composition?

This project searched for direct current evidence of those behaviors. The tested sources did not lock a current, method-auditable direct trade-down finding.

That does not prove that trade-down did not happen.

It only means the direct evidence needed to say it confidently has not been locked.

This matters because pressure is not the same thing as behavior. Higher prices can create pressure, but they do not automatically prove substitution. Business stress can signal strain, but it does not automatically prove household basket changes. Weak demand can suggest softness, but it does not identify which households changed what, when, or why.

So the correct claim is narrow:

**Direct current household trade-down remains unproven.**

The opposite claim is also narrow:

**The evidence does not prove that households avoided trade-down.**

Both overconfident versions should be rejected.

## Why “comfort” fails

The comfort argument has real evidence on its side. It can point to macro resilience, external inflows, formal wage cushioning, and current labor-market context.

But it fails when it tries to turn those facts into a household-wide conclusion.

Formal workers are not all workers. Formal payroll contributors are not all households. Labor-market context is not household income proof. And macro resilience does not automatically mean household comfort.

The evidence can support formal-channel cushioning.

It cannot support broad household comfort.

## Why “collapse” also fails

The collapse argument also has real evidence on its side. It can point to food pressure, tight household margins, uneven public cushioning, small-business financing costs, repayment stress, FX exposure, and demand softness.

But it fails when it turns pressure channels into universal collapse.

Pressure is real, but pressure does not automatically prove total failure. Business stress is important, but it does not directly prove household trade-down. Demand softness matters, but it does not by itself show which households lost purchasing power or how they changed consumption.

The evidence can support selective squeeze.

It cannot support collapse.

## The best current interpretation

The best current interpretation is that the Dominican Republic is experiencing a selective squeeze.

That means:

Macro resilience and household pressure can coexist.

Formal-worker cushioning and informal-worker uncertainty can coexist.

Food pressure and some wage offset can coexist.

Small-business stress and continued economic activity can coexist.

A selective squeeze is not as dramatic as collapse. It is also not as comforting as macro optimism.

It is the middle that the evidence actually supports.

## What would change the conclusion

The conclusion could be strengthened, weakened, or revised if new evidence closes the missing bridges.

The most important missing evidence would be:

1. A current 2026 BCRD or ENCFT income/remuneration table separating formal and informal workers, or showing household income distribution clearly enough to test offset.

2. Current retail scanner or supermarket basket data showing SKU, brand, quantity, unit size, ticket, private-label, or substitution behavior.

3. A current household expenditure-volume survey showing changes in quantities, categories, basket composition, or purchase frequency.

4. A current consumer survey with method details and direct questions about cheaper brands, smaller quantities, substitutes, skipped categories, or reduced consumption.

Until that kind of evidence appears, the responsible conclusion is not to pretend the missing bridge is closed.

## What this article does not claim

This article does not claim that households were broadly cushioned.

It does not claim that informal workers were cushioned.

It does not claim that poor households definitely offset food pressure.

It does not claim that poor households definitely failed to offset food pressure.

It does not claim direct current household trade-down as proven.

It does not claim that direct current household trade-down was absent.

This article does not claim global source absence.

This article does not claim comfort.

This article does not claim collapse.

## Final line

The strongest evidence-bounded conclusion is still the same:

**Not collapse. Not comfort. A selective squeeze.**
